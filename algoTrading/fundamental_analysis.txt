P/E ratio
=========
P/E = Price ÷ Earnings per share
It tells you how many years of earnings the market is willing to pay for the stock.
Example if P = 100, and EPS = 10, P/E = 100/10 = 10 (i.e. you are willing to pay earnings of 10 years upfront).

Another example is lets say you buy a shop on the street for 10 lacs which earns 1 lac every where. so you are paying 10 years of saving upfront to buy the shop.
Typically you do this because you are confident that the earnings will go up in the future. or you think it is a very stable and risk free investment and you can hold it for long term.
i.e.
High P/E → Investors expect strong future growth or view it as a low-risk, high-quality company.

if Price = 10, EPS = 2, P/E ratio = 10/2 = 5. this could mean that either the business is stable and undervalued (so a good stock to buy) or really the business is declining (that's why the market isn't willing to pay more). So Low P/E → Market thinks growth will be slow, earnings are risky, or the business is declining.

So if P/E is high (think is the stock overvalued or is it high because there is a potential growth expected or is it a stable and high quality investment which people hold for long term.
if P/E is low (think is the stock undervalues or is the earnings/revenues/ROIC/NetIncome/EBITDA) declining.

Book Value
==========
Book value is the Total Assets - Total Liabilities i.e. the net asset of a company. It is the value of the company if all its assets are liquidated today.
Book value per share (BVPS) is simply the Book Value divided by the number of outstanding shares.

P/B ratio
=========
Another important metric specially for companies which are asset heavy like Banks (loans and other products are assets), real estate (land is asset) and manufacturing (machinery is asset).
P/B ratio is Price of share divided by the BVPS. its value could be >1, <1, or =1.
Example: a company could have
assets = 10 billion
liabilities = 4 billion
shares in the market = 1 billion
price per share = $8.
Book value = 10 - 4 = 6 billion
BVPS = 6e9/1e9 = $6
P/B ratio = $8/$6 = 1.33
i.e. investors are paying a 33% premium over its fair net asset value to buy the stock. Generally if:
P/B ratio >1, means either the stock is overvalued or the company is in growth phase and investors are willing to pay a premium
P/B ratio <1, means either the stock is undervalued or the company is in distress.
P/B ratio = 1 means a stable, fairly valued.
P/B ratio > 3 means really overvalued. This will be the case when investors think the growth will come in future and not from its current assets (so they buy the stock, driving price and P/B ratio up).

Revenue = 150 * 5 = 750
COGS = 450

Gross Profit --> Simply Revenue - COGS (i.e. only accounts for direct cost associated example $3 per cup is your cost which includes raw materials like cup, beans, syrups etc, milk etc).

Operating Expenses --> includes other associated costs like rent, salaries, R&D, labor costs, deprecation, marketing, utilities etc.

EBIT (Earnings before Interests and Taxes) or Operating Profit = Gross Profit - Operating Expenses

EBITDA (Earnings before Interests and Taxes and Deprecation and Amortization) --> Gross Profit - Operating Expenses + Deprecation + Amortization, i.e. EBIT + Deprecation + Amortization
What is happening is that EBITDA shows a companies core operational performance by excluding financing decisions (interest), tax environments and non-cash accounting charges (deprecation/amortization). So EBITDA measures a companies overall operational profitability and cash flow from core business activities.

Pre Tax income is income after paying out interests. = EBIT - Interests

Net income is income after tax = Pre Tax Income - Taxes

Earnings Per share: net income divided by outstanding shares will give you the earnings per share.

So if revenue = $1000, COGS = $400, Gross Profit is $600. If Operating Expenses = $350 and Deprecation is $50, so EBIT = $600 - $400 = 200.
EBITDA = $200 + Deprecation $50 = $250.
So basically Gross profit of $600 and EBITDA of $250 means you spent $350 as cash flow for the daily run of your business.

Return on Equity (ROE %)
========================
So ROE is an important metric from stakeholder point of view. It tells you how much is the real return on every one dollar of equity that you hold in a company.
ROE% is calculated as Net Income % (total stakeholder equity). Total stakeholder equity is nothing but the book value (i.e. total assets - total liabilities). A high ROE suggests the company is efficient at using its equity base to generate profits. but there is one caveat here. ROE can be artificially inflated by high financial leverage (taking on a lot of debt). A company with massive debt can boost ROE even if its underlying business isn't exceptionally profitable. This makes ROE riskier in isolation. We will see an example below. Always look at ROE and Equity to Debt ratio together.

Return on Invested Capital (ROIC %)
===================================
ROIC measures the return a company generates on ALL the capital invested in it, both debt and equity. It answers the question:  "How effectively does this company use ALL its permanent capital (from both lenders and shareholders) to generate profits from its core operations?"

Formula: ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital

NOPAT means Net Operating Profit After Tax. This is a company's operating profit (EBIT) adjusted for taxes (i.e apply tax on EBIT to get NOPAT). Key: It excludes interest expense/income to remain "capital structure neutral."

Invested Capital: The sum of all debt and equity capital that is "put to work" in the business. A common calculation is: Equity + Interest-bearing Debt - ExcessCash. It represents the total pool of money the business has to operate with.

ROIC is the ultimate measure of capital allocation efficiency. It shows how good management is at investing money (from any source) into the business to generate operating returns. It is unaffected by the company's debt/equity mix, making it excellent for comparing companies across different industries.

ROIC is most meaningful when compared to the company's Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC). If ROIC > WACC, the company is creating value. If ROIC < WACC, it is destroying value, even if it reports a net profit. We will discuss WACC later.

Imagine two identical companies, Company A and Company B, each with $100 in operating assets that generate $15 in annual operating profit (EBIT). The corporate tax rate is 20%.
Company A (No Debt): Financed entirely with $100 of equity.
NOPAT = EBIT x (1 - Tax Rate) = $15 x 0.8 = $12
Net Income = same as NOPAT here, since no interest = $12
ROE = $12 / $100 Equity = 12%
ROIC = $12 NOPAT / $100 Invested Capital = 12%

Company B (Uses Debt): Financed with $50 equity + $50 debt (with a 4% interest rate).
Interest Expense = $50 Debt x 4% = $2.
Net Income = (EBIT - Interest) x (1 - Tax) = ($15 - $2) x 0.8 = $10.4
Shareholders' Equity = $50.
ROE = $10.4 / $50 = 20.8% (Looks much better than Company A!)
ROIC Calculation:
NOPAT is the same as Company A: $12 (We don't subtract interest).
Invested Capital = $50 Equity + $50 Debt = $100.
ROIC = $12 / $100 = 12% (The same as Company A).

The Takeaway: Company B's ROE (20.8%) is much higher than A's (12%), making it look more efficient to shareholders. However, its ROIC (12%) is identical to A's, revealing that their core business operations are equally profitable. The higher ROE is purely a result of using leverage (debt), which adds risk but magnifies equity returns.

PEG Ratio
=========
The P/E-to-Growth (PEG) ratio is a valuation metric that adjusts a company's Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio for its expected earnings growth rate.
Formula: PEG Ratio = (P/E Ratio) ÷ (Annual EPS Growth Rate %)
Core Idea: It attempts to answer: "Is this stock's price justified by its growth prospects?" It puts the P/E ratio into context by considering how fast earnings are growing.
Which P/E ratio to use here (Trailing or Forward):
Trailing P/E: Uses earnings from the past 12 months. More factual.
Forward P/E: Uses estimated future earnings (next 12 months). More common for PEG as it pairs with future growth.
Choice: There's debate, but consistency is key. Most analysts use Forward P/E with the forward growth rate.

Annual EPS Growth rate: This is the estimated annual earnings-per-share (EPS) growth rate, typically over the next 3-5 years. It comes from analyst consensus estimates (e.g., "the Street expects 15% annual EPS growth"). This is the most subjective and critical input.

The pioneer of this metric, Peter Lynch, popularized this framework:
PEG = 1: Theoretically "Fair Value." The stock's price is in perfect equilibrium with its expected growth rate. You're paying $1 for every 1% of growth.
PEG < 1: Potentially Undervalued. The market may be undervaluing the stock's growth prospects. A lower PEG suggests a better "growth-at-a-reasonable-price" (GARP) opportunity.
PEG > 1: Potentially Overvalued. The market is pricing in more growth than analysts project, or there is a premium for quality. A very high PEG (>1.5 or 2) often signals an expensive stock.

Practical Applications & Scenarios. 
Comparing Companies Within an Industry: This is its best use.
-------------------------------------------------------------
Example: Tech Company A has a P/E of 30 and expected growth of 20% → PEG = 1.5.
Tech Company B has a P/E of 25 and expected growth of 25% → PEG = 1.0.
Analysis: Although Company A has a higher P/E, that doesn't tell the whole story. Company B has a lower PEG, suggesting it offers more growth per unit of price paid. It appears to be a better value relative to its growth rate.

Assessing High P/E Stocks: It helps determine if a sky-high P/E is justified.
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
A stock with a P/E of 60 looks extremely expensive. But if its expected growth rate is 50%, its PEG = 1.2, which is only slightly above the "fair value" benchmark. The market is pricing in spectacular growth.

Screening for "GARP" Stocks:
----------------------------
Growth at a Reasonable Price investors use PEG < 1 (or sometimes < 1.5) as a filter to find companies with strong growth that aren't excessively priced.

example Company XYZ:
Current Stock Price: $100
Estimated EPS (Next Year): $5
Forward P/E Ratio: $100 / $5 = 20x
Estimated Annual EPS Growth Rate (Next 5 Years): 15%
PEG Ratio: 20 / 15 = 1.33
Interpretation: A PEG of 1.33 suggests the market is pricing XYZ at a moderate premium to its growth rate (since >1). It may be fairly valued or slightly overvalued unless the company has other strengths (e.g., a bulletproof balance sheet, a moat) that justify the premium. An investor would compare this 1.33 to the PEG ratios of its direct competitors.

Debt-to-Equity-Ratio
=====================
he Debt-to-Equity Ratio (D/E Ratio) is a cornerstone of fundamental analysis that measures a company's financial leverage—essentially, how much it relies on borrowed money versus owned money to finance its assets.

It’s a solvency and leverage ratio that compares a company’s total liabilities to its shareholder equity. It shows the proportion of financing coming from creditors (debt) versus owners (equity).
Formula: D/E Ratio = Total Liabilities ÷ Total Shareholders' Equity
Alternative (More Conservative) Formula: D/E Ratio = (Total Liabilities - Cash & Equivalents) ÷ Shareholders' Equity
This "Net Debt" version is often used in industries where cash is considered non-operational.44There is no single "good" or "bad" D/E ratio. Interpretation depends entirely on context:

Comparison to Industry Averages: This is the most important step.
Capital-Intensive Industries (Utilities, Telecom, Manufacturing, Airlines) naturally have high D/E ratios (often 2.0 or more). They have stable cash flows to service large, predictable debts used to buy expensive assets (power plants, aircraft, factories).

Asset-Light, Tech, or Service Industries (Software, Consulting) typically have low D/E ratios (often < 0.5 or even negative equity). They don't need massive debt to operate.

The Benchmark Rules of Thumb:
D/E > 2.0: Generally considered high risk for most industries (outside of utilities/finance).
D/E = 1.0: A balanced, moderate level of leverage. Debt and equity financing are equal.
D/E < 0.5: Considered conservative/low risk.
D/E = 0: The company has no debt (all-equity financed).
Negative D/E: This occurs when Shareholders' Equity is negative (liabilities exceed assets). This is a major red flag, often called "balance sheet insolvency."
Trend Analysis Over Time:
Is the company's D/E ratio increasing over several quarters? It's taking on more leverage, which could be for expansion (potentially good) or to cover losses (bad).
Is it decreasing? The company is paying down debt or growing equity, becoming less risky.
